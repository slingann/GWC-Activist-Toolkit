We Can Too!

Gender inequality is prevalent in today’s society and will only be remedied if those impacted by it and those contributing to it acknowledge it and work to combat it.

We Can Too! aims to educate and inform users by providing resources and information about gender inequality in STEM and business.

Did you know...

For every 1 company run by a woman, there are 13 companies run by men.

In 2020, 37 of the companies of the Fortune 500 were led by women CEOs, which was a record for the greatest number of companies on the list run by females at the time. Regardless, business management is still male-dominated.

Annual salaries for women in STEM are approximately $15,000 less than annual salaries for men in STEM.

There are additional disparities that stem from race and ethnicity. Black and Hispanic women in STEM earn about $12,000 less than White women in STEM.

Women in STEM and Business

Dr. Ellen Ochoa

“In 1993, Dr. Ellen Ochoa became the first Hispanic woman to go to space when she served on a nine-day mission aboard the space shuttle Discovery. She has flown in space four times, logging nearly 1,000 hours in orbit. Prior to her astronaut career, she was a research engineer and inventor, with three patents for optical systems. Ochoa is also the first Hispanic (and second female) to be named director of NASA’s Johnson Space Center” ("The Untold Story of Women in Science and Technology").

Ada Lovelace

“Ada Lovelace is considered to be the founder of scientific computing and the first computer programmer. Her algorithm — which history has come to know as the first one designed for a machine to carry out — was intended to be used for Charles Babbage’s Analytical Engine, which Lovelace would sadly not see built during her lifetime. Lovelace passed away in 1852, but her previously little-known work and "poetical" approach to science has broken through to inspire present-day young women interested in computer programming” ("The Untold Story of Women in Science and Technology").

Dr. Lisa Su

“Dr. Lisa T.
